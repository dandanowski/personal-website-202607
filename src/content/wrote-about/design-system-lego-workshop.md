<p class="lead">Since it's so easy to create new things in Figma, I had to find a way to communicate why it's worth using what's already available first.</p>

<img src="/uploads/lego-workshop-hero.png" alt="Coffee and Lego workshop" style="display:block;width:100%;border:1px solid var(--line2);border-radius:8px;margin:8px 0 32px">

<p>It's a common challenge: getting product designers to consistently use a design system when building something from scratch in Figma is so easy. Figma is a fantastic tool, but that freedom can undermine consistency across a product team. To tackle this, I built a simple workshop that introduces a little intentional friction to make the value of a design system click.</p>

<h2 id="s1">The activity: building with constraints</h2>
<p>The workshop started first thing in the morning as a warm-up. As people trickled in with coffee, I handed each person a Lego set — I used the <a href="https://www.lego.com/product/mighty-dinosaurs-31058">Lego Creator 3-in-1 Mighty Dinosaurs set (31058)</a>, though any small, contained set would work. They had about an hour to start building, and we'd regroup afterward.</p>
<p>There were a few questioning glances, but most people dug in straight away. The office kitchen had a large table, so everyone naturally gathered around it, chatting about what they were making. Most followed the instructions to build one of the three main models; a few went rogue and built their own thing. After an hour, we reconvened in the meeting room, models and refilled coffee cups in hand, ready to talk about design systems.</p>

<h2 id="s2">Getting the point across</h2>
<p>Lego bricks are a good stand-in for the components in a digital design system — modular, easy to understand, and refittable into countless combinations. That mirrors a well-built component library.</p>
<p>The intentional friction was the point: designers were restricted to what was in the box. They couldn't grab pieces from a different set, and they definitely couldn't 3D-print their own. That limitation directly parallels the time and effort it takes to build a custom component in Figma instead of using one already in the system.</p>
<p>My role, as design-system lead, was to make sure the right pieces were in the box — clear instructions and examples of how the pieces fit together, like a Lego manual. Designers were encouraged to use those examples to build their own models, share them with the team, and inspire each other. If the team collectively decided a crucial piece was missing, it became my job to design and provide it — as modular and easy to use as the rest.</p>
<p>The exercise made "use what's available" easy to see and feel. From that day on, the design system became "the box" in conversation — a comment like "this piece should be in the box" carried a shared meaning everyone on the team instantly understood.</p>

<h2 id="s3">How to improve this workshop</h2>
<ul>
  <li>Add a collaborative challenge. After the first hour of individual building, introduce a task that needs the group to work together — build one large structure using pieces from every set. It surfaces the value of shared components and collaboration.</li>
  <li>Discuss the "why" in more detail. Ask open-ended questions during the debrief: what was easy, what was difficult, did you ever need a piece that wasn't there, how did you solve it? This connects the Lego experience back to their daily work in Figma.</li>
  <li>Create a shared "component library." Before the workshop, pool every set's pieces into one shared container. It better simulates a real design system, where all components are centrally available to the whole team from the start.</li>
</ul>

<p>Have you used an unusual activity to explain a complex topic to your team? I'd love to hear about it.</p>
